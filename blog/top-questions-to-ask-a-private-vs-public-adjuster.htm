Top Questions to Ask a Private vs Public Adjuster

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

The dark, swirling clouds have passed, but the real storm is just beginning. Whether it's the aftermath of a hurricane intensified by climate change, a wildfire fueled by prolonged drought, or a simple basement flood that spiraled out of control, filing an insurance claim is a labyrinth of paperwork, deadlines, and complex policy language. In this moment of vulnerability, you are not alone. You have the right to professional help, but the kind of help you choose can make the difference between a full recovery and a financial catastrophe. This is the critical crossroads: the decision between a public adjuster and the insurance company's adjuster.

Understanding the distinction is not just a matter of semantics; it's about understanding who works for whom. The public adjuster is your hired advocate, paid a percentage of your settlement. The company adjuster, or staff adjuster, is an employee of the insurance carrier. Their primary duty, despite often being well-intentioned, is to protect the financial interests of their employer. Your recovery depends on asking the right questions to both, ensuring you are empowered, not overpowered, in the process.

The Fundamental Divide: Public vs. Company Adjuster

Before we dive into the questions, let's crystallize the roles.

A Public Adjuster is a state-licensed professional who represents you, the policyholder, in preparing and presenting an insurance claim. They work exclusively for your benefit, conducting a detailed assessment of the damage, interpreting your policy's coverage, documenting everything, and negotiating with the insurance company's adjuster on your behalf. Their fee is typically a pre-agreed percentage of the final claim settlement.

A Company Adjuster (or Staff Adjuster) is an employee of your insurance company. They are dispatched to inspect the damage, assess the cost of repairs, and determine how much the company is obligated to pay under the terms of your policy. While they can provide valuable information, it is crucial to remember that their ultimate loyalty lies with the company's bottom line.

Why This Distinction Matters More Than Ever in Today's World

We live in an era of increasing climate volatility. "Unprecedented" weather events are becoming the norm, straining the global insurance industry. Simultaneously, supply chain disruptions and inflation have sent the costs of labor and building materials skyrocketing. In this environment, insurance companies are under immense pressure to minimize payouts to maintain profitability. The meticulously crafted, often opaque, language of your insurance policy is their first line of defense. A public adjuster is your countermeasure—a specialist who speaks the language and knows how to navigate the battlefield of a complex claim.

Your Interrogation Toolkit: Questions for a Public Adjuster

You are considering hiring an advocate. This is a significant financial decision. Do not be shy; be thorough. A reputable public adjuster will welcome these questions.

Questions About Licensing, Experience, and Track Record

  1. "Are you currently licensed in this state, and can I see your license?" This is non-negotiable. Always verify their license status with your state's Department of Insurance.
  2. "How many years have you been a public adjuster, and what is your specific experience with claims like mine?" A adjuster who primarily handles fire damage might not be the best fit for a complex business interruption claim. Look for specialized experience.
  3. "Can you provide references from past clients with claims similar in size and scope to mine?" A proven track record is your best indicator of future performance. Follow up on these references.
  4. "Are you a member of any professional associations, like the National Association of Public Insurance Adjusters (NAPIA)?" Membership often indicates a commitment to ethical standards and ongoing education.

Questions About the Process and Their Strategy

  1. "Walk me through your step-by-step process after you are hired." You need a clear roadmap. How will they document the damage? How will they interact with the insurance company? What will be required of you?
  2. "Based on your initial review, what are the potential strengths and weaknesses of my claim?" A good adjuster will be honest and manage your expectations, not just promise you the moon.
  3. "How do you stay current with local building codes and the true cost of reconstruction in our current inflationary market?" This is critical. Settlements must account for today's prices, not those from two years ago.
  4. "How will you communicate with me, and how often can I expect updates?" Establish communication protocols upfront to avoid anxiety and confusion.

Questions About Fees and the Contract

  1. "What is your fee structure? Is it a fixed percentage, and is it applied to the entire settlement or just the amount you recover above the initial offer?" Fees typically range from 5% to 20%, often on a sliding scale based on the claim size. Get this in writing.
  2. "Does your contract have a 'Retainer' or 'Do Not Compete' clause?" Be wary of any clause that prevents you from speaking directly with your insurance company or that locks you in for an unreasonable period.
  3. "Are there any out-of-pocket expenses I will be responsible for, such as fees for engineers or contractors you hire?" Clarify who pays for these essential third-party experts.
  4. "Under what circumstances can either of us terminate this agreement, and what are the financial implications?" You need a clear exit strategy if the relationship sours.

Navigating the Other Side: Key Questions for the Company Adjuster

The company adjuster is not your enemy, but they are not your friend. Your interactions should be polite, professional, and documented. Your goal is to gather information and understand their position.

Questions to Establish the Scope and Process

  1. "What is your full name, license number, and direct contact information?" This establishes a formal record of your point of contact.
  2. "Can you please outline the entire claims process from start to finish, including all key deadlines?" Understanding their timeline helps you manage your own responsibilities.
  3. "What specific documentation and evidence will you need from me to process this claim efficiently?" This helps you get organized and avoid delays.
  4. "Based on your initial assessment, what areas of my policy do you believe will provide coverage for this loss?" This forces an early discussion about coverage, rather than just the dollar amount.

Probing Questions About the Assessment and Valuation

  1. "How are you accounting for current local building codes in your repair estimates?" Many policies include "Ordinance or Law" coverage, but it must be actively applied. Repairs often must be to current code, which is more expensive.
  2. "Your estimate includes line items for materials and labor. Can you explain how these costs were calculated, and do they reflect the current market rates in our area?" This directly challenges a lowball estimate rooted in outdated cost data.
  3. "Are you considering the full extent of the damage, including what may be hidden behind walls or under floors?" A visual inspection can miss systemic issues. Prompt them to consider destructive testing if necessary.
  4. "For damaged personal property, what methodology are you using to determine Actual Cash Value versus Replacement Cost?" This is a crucial distinction that significantly impacts your payout.

Questions to Protect Your Rights and Understand Your Options

  1. "Can you please point me to the specific policy language and exclusions you are relying on for any part of the claim you are denying or undervaluing?" Do not accept a denial based on a vague reference. Make them show you the exact words.
  2. "If I disagree with your assessment, what is the formal appeals process within the company?" Knowing the chain of command is the first step in escalating a dispute.
  3. "Will all of our communications, including this conversation, be documented in my claim file?" This encourages transparency and accuracy on their part.
  4. "After I receive your written report, what is the timeframe for me to provide my own estimates and documentation for your review?" This keeps the process moving and establishes your proactive role.

The landscape of insurance recovery is fraught with challenges, magnified by global pressures on the industry and the economy. The single most powerful tool you possess is knowledge. By arming yourself with these pointed, strategic questions, you transform from a passive victim into an active, informed participant in your own recovery. Whether you ultimately choose to hire a dedicated champion in a public adjuster or to navigate the process yourself, this interrogation toolkit ensures you do so with your eyes wide open, ready to secure the settlement you have paid for and rightfully deserve. The path to rebuilding begins with asking the right questions.
