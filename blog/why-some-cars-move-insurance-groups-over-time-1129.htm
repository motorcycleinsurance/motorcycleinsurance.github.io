Why Some Cars Move Insurance Groups Over Time

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

The automotive industry is constantly evolving, and so are the factors that determine how much you pay for car insurance. One of the most overlooked yet critical aspects of insurance pricing is how vehicles shift between insurance groups over time. If you’ve ever wondered why your car’s insurance premium suddenly spiked—or dropped—without an obvious reason, the answer often lies in these shifting classifications.

Understanding Insurance Groups

Before diving into why cars move between groups, it’s essential to understand what insurance groups are. In most countries, vehicles are categorized into groups (usually numbered from 1 to 50 or higher) based on risk factors such as:

  • Repair costs – High-end materials and complex engineering make repairs expensive.
  • Performance – Faster cars are statistically more likely to be involved in accidents.
  • Safety features – Advanced driver-assistance systems (ADAS) can lower risk.
  • Theft rates – Some models are targeted more frequently by thieves.

Insurers use these groupings to standardize premiums. A car in Group 5 will generally cost less to insure than one in Group 30. But these classifications aren’t static—they change over time.

Why Do Cars Move Between Insurance Groups?

1. Technological Advancements and Obsolescence

Cars that were once cutting-edge may become outdated as new safety and repair technologies emerge. For example:

  • A 2015 luxury sedan might have been in a high insurance group due to expensive parts. But by 2025, aftermarket components become widely available, lowering repair costs and bumping it down a few groups.
  • Conversely, a car that lacked modern safety features (like automatic emergency braking) might move into a higher-risk group as newer models make it seem comparatively unsafe.

2. Changes in Theft Statistics

Some cars gain notoriety among thieves. If a particular model becomes a frequent target for theft (e.g., due to weak security systems or high black-market demand for parts), insurers may reclassify it into a higher-risk group.

For instance, certain pickup trucks and SUVs are highly sought after in some regions for their parts, leading to increased premiums over time.

3. Market Availability of Parts

If a car manufacturer discontinues a model or stops producing certain components, repair costs can skyrocket due to scarcity. This pushes the vehicle into a higher insurance group.

On the flip side, if third-party manufacturers start producing affordable replacement parts, the same car might drop into a lower group years later.

4. Legislative and Environmental Factors

Governments worldwide are tightening emissions and safety regulations. Cars that don’t meet new standards may face higher insurance costs due to:

  • Stricter crash-test requirements – Older models may be deemed less safe.
  • Emission penalties – Some insurers adjust premiums based on environmental impact.
  • Urban driving restrictions – Cities banning older, polluting cars can increase their risk profile.

5. Accident Data and Claims History

Insurers constantly analyze real-world data. If a particular model starts showing higher-than-expected accident rates (perhaps due to a newly discovered design flaw), its insurance group may rise.

For example, if a sports car’s braking system is found to underperform in wet conditions, insurers might reassess its risk level.

Real-World Examples of Insurance Group Shifts

Tesla’s Rollercoaster Ride

Tesla vehicles have seen significant insurance group fluctuations due to:

  • High repair costs – Early models had expensive aluminum bodies and proprietary parts, placing them in high-risk groups.
  • Software updates improving safety – Over-the-air updates enhanced Autopilot, reducing accident rates in some cases.
  • Battery fire concerns – Early reports of battery fires temporarily raised premiums before safety improvements were implemented.

The Rise (and Fall) of Diesel Cars

In the early 2010s, diesel cars were often in lower insurance groups due to fuel efficiency. But after the Dieselgate scandal and subsequent emissions crackdowns, many diesel models were reclassified into higher-risk categories due to:

  • Increased maintenance costs from stricter emissions testing.
  • Declining resale value, making them less desirable.

How to Stay Ahead of Insurance Group Changes

If you’re buying a car—or already own one—here’s how to anticipate (and possibly mitigate) insurance group shifts:

Research Historical Group Trends

Some car review sites track insurance group changes over time. If a model has been steadily moving up, it might continue.

Opt for Models with Strong Aftermarket Support

Cars with widely available third-party parts tend to stay in lower insurance groups longer.

Consider Future-Proof Safety Features

Vehicles with upgradable safety tech (like Tesla’s Autopilot) may avoid sharp premium hikes.

Monitor Legislative Changes

Upcoming emissions or safety laws could impact your car’s classification. Staying informed helps avoid surprises.

The Bottom Line

Insurance groups aren’t set in stone—they evolve with technology, market trends, and real-world data. A car that’s cheap to insure today might not be in five years, and vice versa. By understanding the forces behind these shifts, you can make smarter decisions when buying, maintaining, or insuring a vehicle.
